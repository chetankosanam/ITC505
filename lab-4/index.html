FL-XGBTC: Federated Learning Inspired with XGBoost Tuned Classifier for YouTube Spam Content Detection

Abstract

The problem of spam content in YouTube comments is an ongoing issue, and detecting such content is a critical task to maintain the quality of user experience on the platform. In this study, we propose a Federated Learning Inspired XG-Boost Tuned Classifier, FL-XGBTC, for YouTube spam content detection. The proposed model leverages the advantages of federated learning, which enables the training of a model collaboratively across multiple devices without sharing raw data. The FL-XGBTC model is based on the XGBoost algorithm, a powerful and widely used ensemble learning algorithm for classification tasks.

Keywords

Federated learning, NLP, Machine learning, YouTube, Sentiment analysis, Classifiers, Ham, Spam

Introduction

Because YouTube is extremely popular, spammers have increasingly utilized its comments section to spread spam, raising concerns about phishing attacks that target users. Spam detection on YouTube still requires improvements. A five-stage framework for identifying YouTube spam has been proposed (Abdulhamid et al. 2017), including data collection, pre-processing, feature selection, extraction, classification, and detection. YouTube, a platform founded in 2005 and acquired by Google in 2006, allows users to follow, comment on, and subscribe to channels. The detection of spam on this platform is crucial to maintaining user trust and safety.

Related Work

Our classifier has been contrasted with various other data mining methods, showing an accuracy rate of 84.3% when identifying inappropriate content aimed at younger audiences. Using grid-search cross-validation for tuning hyperparameters, we improved classification performance. Our classifier addresses the risks associated with toddlers encountering harmful content on YouTube. Prior work (Gothankar et al. 2021; Gourisaria et al. 2022) highlighted the shortcomings of existing detection systems, which this work aims to improve.

Mathematical Modeling of Proposed Approach

In federated learning, 1000 iterations were performed, with each local node training a unique model using its dataset across five rounds. After each iteration, a copy of the global model was shared with all participating nodes. Local losses and accuracies were monitored throughout the training process. The shared model, built on client contributions, helped improve classification accuracy without sharing sensitive data (Concone et al. 2022; Ezpeleta et al. 2018).

Dataset

The dataset used for spam classification was provided by Alberto, Lochter, and Almeida (2015), which collected YouTube comments from top-viewed videos in 2015. Comments were manually labeled as spam or legitimate, providing a rich dataset for evaluating classification algorithms. Videos like Psy's "Gangnam Style" were included, and the dataset was accessed via the YouTube API for further analysis.

Training and Testing Process

We applied TF-IDF to vectorize the comments and split the dataset into 80% training and 20% testing. Several machine learning algorithms were evaluated for performance, with Random Forest achieving 94% accuracy and 95% precision. Decision Tree performed with 91% accuracy and 97% precision, while logistic regression achieved a 91% accuracy and 96% precision. These results demonstrate the efficiency of these models for binary classification problems.

Results and Discussion

Detecting spam based solely on content remains challenging due to the nuances of language. Recent advances in processing power have allowed machine learning techniques to address these challenges. Our contribution focuses on improving classifier performance, particularly in identifying spam content, with a focus on increasing the F1 score. Key steps included cleaning data, conducting exploratory data analysis, building models, and deploying the final solution.

Conclusion

The study emphasizes the importance of the federated learning framework proposed in FL-XGBTC for YouTube spam content detection. By incorporating XGBoost as a classifier and leveraging a decentralized learning approach, the study showcases significant improvements in spam detection. The use of federated learning ensures that privacy is maintained while improving classifier accuracy and precision, making the FL-XGBTC model a viable solution for addressing the challenges of detecting spam in a dynamic online environment.
